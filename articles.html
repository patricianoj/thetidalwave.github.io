AfterSchool App

By: Patricia Ornelas | January 2016 | Richmond, CA

School gossip, rumors, and crushes are now at the touch of your fingertips with AfterSchool. This app is a forum for high school students to express themselves through anonymous posts (sometimes accompanied with a photo) to their school’s feed. The free app is meant to provide students with a space to communicate ideas and thoughts without judgment.

With an estimated two million to 10 million users, the app has spread to more than 22,000 high school campuses. Since it is a teenagers-only social media application, each user must provide proof of where he or she attends high school. This verification is made from the user’s Facebook profile. Then, users can read AfterSchool posts from their high school only. As a result of these protections, most adults never see the posts or even become aware of the app.

Early on, the app was seen as dangerous. In fact, there was even a gun threat in December 2014 that brought on quite the scare to a Michigan high school. After that, the app’s creators tightened up on security and made the app much more safe by limiting what could be posted by filtering keywords that involve violence or profanity. Students found loopholes to some of these security measures by replacing profanity with suggestive emojis.

At Making Waves Academy, use of the app has taken an unexpected turn. As one student put it, “People just profess their love for others on there.” Rather than being used for harm, the app has turned into a secret-holder for students to discuss their crushes.

Of course, this has also had negative consequences. As expected, some students feel uncomfortable about someone fostering feelings for them and making those feelings public. Sometimes, the posts go as far as making harassing statements about another student’s body or physical appearance. It can be overwhelming for a student to read that a fellow student sees them in that way.

“It’s honestly just creepy,” said Yulissa Espejo, a student at Making Waves.

Interestingly, the fact that postings are anonymous makes for unanswered questions that circulate through the halls of Making Waves. A heightened sense of mystery follows posts on the AfterSchool app.

Some students think that it is necessary to have a place for free expression. “People just love hiding behind a screen,” said Ivan Jauregui, another Making Waves student.

As a public forum solely for high school students, the app is useful for complimenting others and reviewing entertainment. It provides relatable posts such as, “I don’t understand how my room gets so messy when I literally sit in one spot with my laptop all day. Additionally, the posts can provide confidence boosts such as, “I’m scared to talk to Anderson -- he’s so cute.”

While AfterSchool has raised many concerns regarding the safety of students and credibility of messages, it has become an entertaining source about he latest information on student social life.
